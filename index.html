New kinds of social coordination: three typologies

In Silicon Valley 'sharing economy' startups get huge valuations. In London, big corporates are opening 'innovation lofts' and coworking hubs, struggling to work out what the AirBnB of washing powder is.

A subgenre of books starting with Clay Shirky's Here Come Everybody, most recently exemplified in Paul Mason's Postcapitalism all describe new, digitally facilitated economic structures.

Underlying it all is the idea of social coordination: organising people and resources to achieve collective goals. Historically, markets and governments predominated.

But the web enables hybrid, even completely new, kinds of social coordination. This page presents three ways to categorise emergent species of social coordination.

Not all digital phenomena are about social coordination. I didn't include Udacity (a mooc), it's a digital incarnation of the conventional university structure. I did include Peer To Peer University, because it has a fresh approach to generating educational material, a new way of connecting supply and demand.

Resource Structure: public, private, club, commons

When Economists think about social coordination, they often think about the intrinsic properties of the resource being shared. How hard is it to stop others using it - is it excludable? If someone else uses it, does it run out - is it rivalrous?

Excludable Non-excludable
Rivalrous Private Goods
(clothes / food)		 Common Pool Resources
(common land / fisheries)
Non-rivalrous Club Goods
(gym / university)		 Public Goods
(Street lights / national defense)

So the classic analysis gives rise to four kinds of goods, each of which, economists would predict, are most effectively provided by different kinds of organisation. For example it will be hard for a for profit company to provide street lighting, while a government, it's though, is bad at providing clothing or food.

It's intrinsic to, for example, a fishing ground that its hard to prevent others from fishing on it - you can't build a fence round it. If one person catches the fish, another person cannot. This makes a fishery a common pool resource.

Many digital services are non-rivalrous - one person consuming information from Wikipedia does not diminish the amount of information left for others, yet they do require constant management, in this example the wikipedians who curate the information.

However, the decision to be exclusionary is not intrinsic to the nature of the resource. You can put a password on a web-based resource, or not - it's up to the designers.

Elinor Ostrom's notion of a knowledge commons captures exactly these ambiguities in an extra, fifth category. For this analysis I've placed Club Goods and Common Pool Resources in one column, and made a special column for the (extremely prevalent) knowledge commons.

Exchange Mechanic

One way to consider systems of digital social coordination is how exchanges are mediated, are they voluntary, for money, or... in Bitcoin? Some other digital store of value?

I haven't gone into Bitcoin in a big way here, which isn't itself an example of digital social coordination, but instead a platform that could cause it. Augur, the prediction market, is the only cryptocurrency example included - because it's such an interesting idea from so many perspectives.

However, the notion of tracking value in a community is extremely important for preventing abuse.

In some projects, such as Stack Overflow there are points based psuedo-currencies. In others, such as Kahn academy, most of the value is created centrally and donated to the community. In others, such as Peer To Peer University, participants can all create and consume.

Spare Capacity

Robin Chase's new book Peers Inc makes the case that the use of 'spare capacity' is the critical component of new kinds of social coordination. The startup she co-founded, Zipcar, is exactly about spare capacity, allowing people to share their car when it's not in use. She's currently working on BlaBlaCar, a ride sharing platform for car journeys.

It's clearly the case that some news kinds of social coordination are about using the Internet's information sharing power to make more efficient use of existing resources. AirBnB allows you to rent out a room that simply isn't worth the hassle, Etsy might enable someone to use their spare time more profitably.

Peers Inc perhaps makes this claim a little too broadly though, I'm not sure for example that a smartphone's capacity to install apps really counts as spare capacity for example.

I've considered the extent to which this applies to the projects listed, and put each on into one of four categories accordingly.
